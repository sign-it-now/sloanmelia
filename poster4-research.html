Research Methods —
Study Designs, Validity & Ethics

Every claim in psychology — including every forensic expert opinion — rests on research. Understanding how that research was designed tells you how much to trust the conclusion.

01

Research Design Types

The architecture of every study you will read
Strongest Causal Evidence
True Experiment (RCT)

Participants randomly assigned to conditions. Experimenter controls the independent variable. Gold standard for establishing cause-and-effect. Random assignment eliminates most confounds.

StrengthsEstablishes causation. Controls for confounds. Replicable.
WeaknessesOften impossible ethically. Low ecological validity. Expensive.
Randomly assign offenders to CBT vs. no treatment; measure recidivism rates at 2 years.
Moderate Causal Evidence
Quasi-Experiment

Resembles an experiment but lacks true random assignment. Uses pre-existing groups or natural circumstances to compare conditions. Common in forensic research where randomization is impossible.

StrengthsMore feasible. Higher external validity than lab experiments.
WeaknessesCannot fully rule out confounds. Selection bias risk.
Compare recidivism rates between inmates who voluntarily enrolled in treatment vs. those who did not.
Association — No Causation
Correlational Study

Measures the relationship between two or more variables without manipulating either. Establishes that variables co-vary, not that one causes the other. Generates hypotheses for experimental testing.

StrengthsEasy to conduct. Large samples. Naturalistic. Generates hypotheses.
WeaknessesCannot establish causation. Third variable problem.
PCL-R scores and violent recidivism rates correlate at r = 0.27 across studies — meaningful but not deterministic.
Observation Only
Case Study

In-depth analysis of a single individual or small group. Rich, detailed data. Cannot establish causation or generalize findings. Historically important in psychology (Phineas Gage, Henry Molaison).

StrengthsExtreme depth. Rare phenomena. Hypothesis generation.
WeaknessesNo generalizability. No causation. Researcher bias risk.
Detailed forensic case study of John Wayne Gacy to understand organized serial killer personality structure.
Population Snapshot
Survey / Longitudinal Study

Surveys measure variables at one point in time. Longitudinal studies follow the same participants over time — years or decades — to track development. Best for developmental and risk factor research.

StrengthsTracks change over time. Establishes temporal sequence. Large samples.
WeaknessesExpensive. Attrition. Cannot establish causation alone.
The Cambridge Study in Delinquent Development — followed 411 London boys from age 8 to 50 to identify risk factors for persistent offending.
Lived Experience
Qualitative Research

Non-numerical data — interviews, observations, focus groups, document analysis. Captures meaning, experience, and context that numbers miss. Often used to develop theory or understand new phenomena.

StrengthsRich depth. Captures subjective experience. Theory generation.
WeaknessesNot generalizable. Researcher bias. Not quantifiable.
In-depth interviews with exonerated individuals about their interrogation experience to understand coerced confession dynamics.
02

Validity — The Four Types Every Researcher Must Know

What makes a study trustworthy?
Internal Validity
Causal Confidence

Does the study actually measure the effect of the independent variable — or is something else causing the result? High internal validity = confident causation claim.

Threats: Selection bias, history effects, maturation, instrumentation changes, demand characteristics.

External Validity
Generalizability

Can findings be applied beyond the sample to other people, settings, and times? Lab studies often sacrifice external validity for internal validity.

Threats: Volunteer bias, artificial settings, WEIRD samples (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic).

Construct Validity
Measuring What You Claim

Does your measure actually measure the construct it claims to? A "violence risk" scale must actually predict violence — not just antisocial attitudes.

Threats: Poor operationalization, measuring proxies instead of the actual construct, criterion contamination.

Statistical Conclusion Validity
Math Integrity

Were the correct statistical tests used? Was the sample large enough? Are the conclusions proportionate to the data? P-values alone don't tell the whole story.

Threats: Insufficient power, violated assumptions, p-hacking, multiple comparisons without correction.

Test Validity Types
Content Validity

Does the test cover all relevant aspects of the construct? A psychopathy measure that only assesses behavior lacks the interpersonal/affective content validity to measure the full construct.

Assessed by expert panel review. Essential for forensic tool admissibility under Daubert standard.

Test Validity Types
Predictive Validity

Does the test predict what it claims to predict? The PCL-R has predictive validity if high scores actually correlate with violent recidivism in follow-up studies.

Assessed by correlating test scores with criterion outcomes at a later time point. AUC is the key statistic.

Reliability
Inter-Rater Reliability

Do two independent raters arrive at the same score? Critical for tools like the PCL-R where scoring depends on clinical judgment. Measured by Cohen's Kappa (≥0.70 required).

Low inter-rater reliability undermines admissibility in court. Training and structured administration protocols improve reliability.

Reliability
Test-Retest Reliability

Does the same person score similarly if tested twice over time? Important for stable traits. Risk tools measuring dynamic factors expect some score change — that's the point.

Time interval, learning effects, and genuine trait change all affect test-retest reliability interpretation.

Research Ethics — The Non-Negotiables

The APA code and IRB requirements every researcher must know
Belmont Report Principle
Informed Consent

Participants must be fully informed of risks, benefits, and their right to withdraw at any time without penalty — before they agree to participate.

Incarcerated populations are considered vulnerable — additional protections required. Consent obtained under coercion is not valid consent.
Belmont Report Principle
Beneficence / Non-Maleficence

Research must maximize potential benefits and minimize potential harms. Researchers have an obligation to protect participants from harm.

Forensic evaluations involve unique tension: the evaluator serves the court, not the defendant. This creates role conflict that must be disclosed.
Belmont Report Principle
Justice / Equity

Benefits and burdens of research must be distributed fairly across society. Historically disadvantaged groups must not bear disproportionate research risk.

Historical abuses (Tuskegee, prison experiments) led to current protections. IRB oversight is mandatory for all human subjects research.
Research Practice
Confidentiality / Limits

Research data must be kept confidential. In forensic contexts, confidentiality has legal limits — duty to warn (Tarasoff) and mandatory reporting obligations override confidentiality.

Forensic evaluees must be told upfront that the evaluation is not confidential and will be shared with the court. This fundamentally changes the evaluative relationship.
Research Practice
Deception & Debriefing

Deception is sometimes permitted in research if: the study cannot be done otherwise, risks are minimal, and participants are debriefed fully afterward.

Classic studies (Milgram, Zimbardo) would not pass modern IRB review. The field's ethical standards have evolved significantly since the 1960s–70s.
Publication Ethics
Replication Crisis

Many classic psychology findings (ego depletion, power poses, priming effects) have failed to replicate. Forensic testimony based on non-replicated findings is ethically problematic.

Expert witnesses have an obligation to represent the state of the science accurately — including its limitations. Overclaiming findings is an ethical violation under APA guidelines.

When to Use Which Statistical Test

The researcher's quick decision guide
Compare 2 Group Means
Independent t-test

Use when: Comparing means of two separate groups on a continuous variable. E.g., Do high-PCL-R scorers recidivate faster than low scorers?

Compare 3+ Group Means
ANOVA

Use when: Comparing means across three or more groups. E.g., Do low, medium, and high-risk groups differ in recidivism rates?

Categorical Data
Chi-Square Test

Use when: Comparing frequencies or proportions. E.g., Is the proportion of white male offenders greater than expected from population demographics?

Two Continuous Variables
Pearson Correlation

Use when: Measuring the linear relationship between two continuous variables. E.g., Does PCL-R score correlate with number of prior convictions?

Predict from Variables
Regression Analysis

Use when: Predicting an outcome from one or more predictor variables. E.g., Which factors best predict violent recidivism in a parole sample?

Predict Binary Outcome
Logistic Regression

Use when: Predicting a yes/no outcome. E.g., What factors predict whether an individual will reoffend (yes/no) within 5 years?

Diagnostic Accuracy
ROC / AUC Analysis

Use when: Evaluating how well a tool distinguishes between two groups (recidivists vs. non-recidivists). Standard for forensic risk tool validation research.

Rater Agreement
Cohen's Kappa

Use when: Measuring agreement between two raters beyond what chance predicts. Required to establish reliability of clinical rating tools like PCL-R for court use.

Synthesize Many Studies
Meta-Analysis

Use when: Combining results across many studies to estimate the overall effect size. The strongest form of evidence in evidence-based practice and Daubert admissibility arguments.

Time to Event
Survival Analysis

Use when: Measuring how long until an event occurs (e.g., time to first reoffense after release). Accounts for participants who haven't yet experienced the event at study end.
