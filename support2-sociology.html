Sociology & Criminology —
Crime as a Social Phenomenon

Individual psychology explains why one person commits a crime. Sociology explains why crime rates differ across neighborhoods, races, economic classes, and historical periods. Without sociology, forensic psychologists miss half the picture — and get basic facts about criminal behavior wrong.

01

Major Criminological Theories

The sociological frameworks that explain criminal behavior at the population level
Social Strain Theory
Merton's Anomie Theory (1938)

Society promotes success goals (wealth, status) but provides unequal access to legitimate means. Those blocked from legitimate paths adapt: conformity, innovation (crime), ritualism, retreatism, or rebellion. Crime is a rational adaptation to structural inequality.

Explains why crime rates are elevated in low-income communities — not because of individual moral failure but because legitimate pathways to culturally-valued goals are systematically blocked. Essential context in forensic social history and sentencing mitigation.
Social Learning Theory
Sutherland's Differential Association

Criminal behavior is learned through intimate personal groups. People become criminal when they are exposed to more definitions favorable to law violation than against it. Crime is learned the same way any other behavior is learned.

Gang membership, family criminality, and criminal peer associations are the primary social learning mechanisms for criminal behavior. This theory is foundational for juvenile risk assessment and explains why peer group intervention is the most cost-effective delinquency prevention strategy.
Social Control Theory
Hirschi's Bonds to Society (1969)

Everyone has criminal impulses — what varies is the strength of the social bond that prevents acting on them. Four bond elements: Attachment (caring about others' opinions), Commitment (investment in conventional life), Involvement (time in conventional activities), Belief (acceptance of moral validity of law).

Weak social bonds are among the most reliable predictors of recidivism. Risk assessment tools (LSI-R) directly measure bond strength — employment, family ties, prosocial activities, and conventional beliefs. Strengthening bonds is the mechanism of effective rehabilitation programs.
Labeling Theory
Becker, Lemert — Secondary Deviance

Being officially labeled as deviant (arrested, convicted, incarcerated) changes how others respond to a person — and how that person sees themselves. Secondary deviance is criminal behavior that results from internalizing the deviant label.

Explains why arrest and incarceration can increase rather than reduce future offending — especially in adolescents. Diversion programs, expungement laws, and ban-the-box employment policies are all policy responses to labeling theory findings. Forensic evaluators should document how criminal justice involvement itself has affected a defendant's trajectory.
Routine Activities Theory
Cohen & Felson (1979)

Crime occurs when three elements converge: a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of a capable guardian. Crime rate changes are explained by changes in daily routines — not changes in individual motivation.

Relevant in victimology evaluations and crime scene analysis. Explains crime clustering in specific locations and times. Used in threat assessment to identify when and where violence is most likely and what environmental modifications reduce risk.
Life Course Criminology
Sampson & Laub — Age-Graded Theory

Criminal behavior follows predictable developmental trajectories. "Turning points" — marriage, employment, military service — can redirect criminal careers even for chronic offenders. Social bonds developed in adulthood matter as much as childhood risk factors.

The most empirically supported criminological framework for forensic risk assessment. Explains why dynamic risk factors (current employment, current relationship stability) are as important as static historical factors in violence risk evaluation.
