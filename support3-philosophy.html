Philosophy — Logic & Ethics
The Most Underrated Course on the List

Forensic psychology sits at the intersection of science and law — and both fields run on formal logical argument. A forensic evaluator who cannot identify a fallacy in an attorney's cross-examination, or reason clearly about competing ethical obligations, is dangerously underprepared for the courtroom.

01

Logical Fallacies Every Forensic Psychologist Must Recognize

Attorneys use these in cross-examination. You need to spot them.
FallacyWhat It IsCourtroom ExampleHow to Respond
Ad HominemAttacking the person rather than their argument or evidence."Dr. Smith testified for the prosecution in 90% of cases — clearly biased." (Does not address the actual findings.)Redirect to the data. "My findings are based on standardized assessment, not personal opinion."
False DichotomyPresenting only two options when more exist."Either this defendant is fully sane or fully insane — which is it, doctor?"Explain that legal sanity is a continuum with multiple components, not a binary switch.
Straw ManMisrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack."So you're saying that anyone who has PTSD can't be held responsible for violence?""That is not what I said. What I said was..." — restate the actual opinion clearly.
Post HocAssuming that because B followed A, A caused B."The defendant watched violent video games, then committed violence. Therefore the games caused the violence."Correlation is not causation. Multiple variables intervene. Base rates matter.
Appeal to AuthorityClaiming something is true because an authority said it — without evidence."The FBI says most serial killers are white males, so the profile must be valid."Authority is not evidence. What does the peer-reviewed data say? What are the base rates?
Ecological FallacyApplying group-level statistics to individual cases without justification."Studies show this risk group recidivates at 40% — therefore this defendant will recidivate."Group statistics describe probabilities, not individual destinies. Individual factors modify group-level predictions.
Slippery SlopeClaiming one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without evidence for the causal chain."If we accept PTSD as a mitigating factor, every veteran will claim PTSD to avoid accountability."Each case is evaluated individually on its specific evidence. Policy concerns don't determine individual clinical findings.
02

Major Ethical Frameworks — Applied to Forensic Psychology

The philosophical foundations of the APA Ethics Code
Consequentialism
Utilitarian Ethics — Mill & Bentham

The morally right action is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Outcomes determine rightness. The consequences of a decision — not the decision itself — carry moral weight.

When a forensic evaluator must decide whether to breach confidentiality to protect a third party (Tarasoff duty to warn), utilitarian reasoning asks: does warning the potential victim produce more overall good than the harm of breaching confidentiality? This is the philosophical foundation of the duty-to-warn framework.
Deontological Ethics
Kant's Categorical Imperative

Some actions are inherently right or wrong regardless of consequences. Act only according to rules you would want universalized. Treat people as ends in themselves, never merely as means. Duties and rights are primary.

Informed consent is a deontological requirement — it is owed to defendants regardless of whether getting it produces better outcomes. The forensic evaluator who deceives an examinee to get "better" data violates a categorical duty even if the data helps the case. Rules-based ethics underpin most professional codes.
Virtue Ethics
Aristotle — Character and Flourishing

The focus is not on rules or consequences but on the character of the moral agent. What would a person of good character do? Virtues — honesty, courage, justice, practical wisdom — guide action. Ethics is about who you are, not just what you do.

When an attorney pressures a forensic evaluator to shade their findings, virtue ethics asks: what does an honest, courageous professional do here? The APA's aspirational principles (integrity, beneficence, justice) are written in the language of virtue ethics — describing the kind of psychologist one should strive to be.
Principlism
Beauchamp & Childress — Biomedical Ethics Framework

Four principles guide biomedical and psychological ethics: Autonomy (respect persons' self-determination), Beneficence (do good), Non-maleficence (do no harm), Justice (distribute benefits and burdens fairly). When principles conflict, context determines priority.

Principlism is the framework underlying the Belmont Report and most research ethics training. Forensic psychology routinely involves conflicts between these principles — a defendant's autonomy vs. public safety, an evaluatee's wellbeing vs. the court's need for truthful testimony. Understanding how to reason through these conflicts is the mark of an ethical practitioner.
Free Will vs. Determinism
The Core Philosophical Question of Criminal Justice

Did the defendant freely choose to act, or were they determined by factors outside their control — genetics, brain injury, childhood trauma, mental illness? The answer has profound implications for criminal responsibility, punishment, and rehabilitation.

Every criminal responsibility evaluation engages this question. Hard determinists argue no one is truly responsible for anything. Compatibilists argue free will and determinism can coexist. The law assumes a compatibilist position — people are responsible unless a specific impairment substantially undermined their cognitive or volitional control. Forensic evaluators must be able to articulate this framework clearly under cross-examination.
Philosophy of Science
What Counts as Knowledge? Popper's Falsifiability

Karl Popper argued that genuine science produces falsifiable claims — claims that could in principle be proven wrong. A hypothesis that can explain any outcome is not scientific. Science advances by attempting to falsify hypotheses, not confirm them.

Daubert v. Merrell Dow (1993) essentially codified Popper in federal law — expert testimony must be based on testable, falsifiable science with known error rates. A forensic evaluator whose methods cannot be falsified — "clinical intuition" with no structured protocol — cannot survive Daubert challenge. Philosophy of science is directly admissibility-relevant.
