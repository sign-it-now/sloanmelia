Cognitive Biases — Full Map
Testimony, Juries & Investigations

Cognitive biases are systematic errors in thinking that affect judgment and decision-making in everyone — including investigators, evaluators, attorneys, jurors, and witnesses. The forensic justice system is vulnerable to all of them. Knowing these biases is knowing where justice fails.

Category 01

Memory & Perception Biases

How we remember — and misremember — what we witness
Misinformation Effect

Post-event information contaminates and alters the original memory. Loftus's foundational research. Leading questions create false memories that witnesses report as genuine.

Asking "Did you see the broken headlight?" implants the memory of a headlight even if none existed.
Eyewitness · Interview Protocol · Expert Testimony
Source Monitoring Error

Misattributing the origin of a memory — confusing whether something was actually experienced, imagined, seen on TV, or told to you by another person.

A child who was told about an event may later genuinely believe they witnessed it.
Child Testimony · False Memory · Suggestibility
Weapon Focus Effect

The presence of a weapon narrows attention — witnesses focus on the weapon and have significantly reduced accuracy identifying the perpetrator's face, clothing, or other details.

Armed robbery witnesses accurately describe the gun but cannot reliably identify the shooter.
Eyewitness Reliability · Cross-Examination
Cross-Race Effect

People are significantly more accurate at recognizing and distinguishing faces of their own racial group than those of other racial groups. Well-documented and robust finding.

A white witness to a crime involving Black perpetrators will have lower identification accuracy than for white perpetrators, all else equal.
Eyewitness Identification · Wrongful Convictions
Confidence-Accuracy Dissociation

Eyewitness confidence is a poor predictor of eyewitness accuracy. High-confidence witnesses are often wrong. Jurors dramatically overweight witness confidence in their credibility assessments.

A witness who says "I am absolutely certain it was him" is not statistically more accurate than one who expresses uncertainty.
Jury Decision-Making · Expert Testimony
Hindsight Bias

After learning an outcome, people believe they "knew it all along" and overestimate how predictable the outcome was beforehand. Retrospective probability distortion.

After a violent act, everyone concludes the person was "obviously" dangerous and that risk was "clear."
Criminal Responsibility · Risk Assessment · Expert Witness
Category 02

Investigative & Evaluator Biases

How investigators and evaluators contaminate their own conclusions
Confirmation Bias

Seeking, interpreting, and remembering information in a way that confirms pre-existing beliefs. The single most dangerous bias in forensic investigation and evaluation.

An investigator who suspects a specific person as guilty focuses on evidence against them and discounts or ignores exculpatory evidence.
Investigation · Profiling · Wrongful Conviction
Anchoring Bias

Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making subsequent judgments. Adjustments from the anchor are typically insufficient.

A forensic evaluator told upfront that a defendant "is a psychopath" will interpret all subsequent data through that lens.
Forensic Evaluation · Blind Assessment Protocol
Tunnel Vision

Focusing narrowly on a single hypothesis or suspect while failing to adequately consider alternatives. A specific combination of confirmation bias plus anchoring in investigative contexts.

DC Sniper case — investigators focused on a single white male suspect profile while actual perpetrators drove through checkpoints.
Investigation · Profiling · Missing Suspect
Outcome Bias

Judging a past decision by its outcome rather than by the quality of the decision at the time it was made. A specific form of hindsight bias applied to professional conduct.

A parole board that releases an offender who later reoffends is criticized, even if the release decision was statistically sound at the time.
Risk Assessment · Parole · Expert Testimony
Expectation Bias

An evaluator's prior expectations influence their observation, interpretation, and documentation of data. Even experienced clinicians are susceptible to finding what they expect to find.

A forensic evaluator referred a case by the prosecution unconsciously frames ambiguous data in ways consistent with guilt.
Forensic Evaluation Ethics · Blind Assessment
Role Effect / Adversarial Allegiance

Forensic experts systematically reach conclusions favorable to the side that retained them — even when using the same data. Research demonstrates this bias is robust and largely unconscious.

Defense-retained and prosecution-retained experts reading identical records reach different PCL-R scores at statistically significant rates.
Expert Testimony · Ethics · Daubert
Category 03

Jury & Decision-Making Biases

How jurors decide — and what actually drives verdicts
Halo Effect

One positive (or negative) attribute of a person influences judgments about their other, unrelated attributes. Attractiveness, articulateness, and race all trigger halo effects in jurors.

Attractive defendants receive lighter sentences on average. Well-dressed defendants are perceived as more credible.
Jury Research · Sentencing Disparity
Attribution Error (FAE)

Overattributing behavior to dispositional (internal, character) factors while underestimating situational factors. Jurors over-attribute criminal behavior to character and underweight circumstances.

"He did it because he's a bad person" — ignoring poverty, abuse history, mental illness, or coercion.
Jury Deliberation · Mitigation · Sentencing
Just World Belief

The tendency to believe the world is fundamentally fair — that people get what they deserve. Victims are subtly blamed to maintain this belief. Produces blame-the-victim thinking in sexual assault cases.

"She must have done something to provoke it" — preserving the belief that bad things don't happen to good people.
Sexual Assault Cases · Victim Credibility · Jury Bias
Implicit Association / Racial Bias

Unconscious associations linking race with criminality, danger, or culpability. Demonstrated through IAT research. Operates below conscious awareness in jurors, investigators, and evaluators.

Black defendants receive harsher sentences than white defendants for equivalent crimes across jurisdictions and controlling for criminal history.
Sentencing Disparity · Jury Selection · Death Penalty
Story Model of Juror Decision-Making

Jurors don't weigh evidence atomically — they construct a narrative story of what happened and then evaluate evidence for how well it fits that story. The most coherent story wins, not the most evidence.

The prosecution's narrative "he planned it and executed it coldly" beats scattered defense evidence if the story is more coherent.
Trial Strategy · Expert Communication · Jury Research
CSI Effect

Jurors raised on forensic TV expect scientific evidence in every case. Cases without DNA, fingerprints, or forensic lab results are perceived as weak — regardless of the strength of non-scientific evidence.

Jurors acquit despite strong circumstantial evidence because "there was no DNA." Or convict based on forensic evidence that's actually contested science.
Jury Expectations · Expert Testimony · Forensic Evidence
Category 04

Reasoning & Judgment Biases

How all humans — including experts — reason poorly under uncertainty
Availability Heuristic

Judging the probability of an event based on how easily an example comes to mind. Vivid, recent, or emotionally salient examples are overweighted.

After a high-profile mass shooting, public perception of mass shooting frequency dramatically increases — regardless of actual statistical rate.
Risk Perception · Media Influence · Policy
Representativeness Heuristic

Judging probability based on how closely something resembles a prototype. Ignores base rates in favor of stereotypical matching. Underlies criminal profiling errors.

Assuming someone matches the "type" of perpetrator without base rate analysis — exactly the DC Sniper error.
Profiling · Base Rate Neglect · Investigation
Base Rate Neglect

Ignoring prior probability (base rate) when evaluating new information. The most statistically significant error in forensic decision-making. Profiles without base rate grounding are guesswork.

If a risk tool identifies someone as "high risk" with 70% accuracy, but only 5% of people commit violent crimes, most high-risk designations are still false positives.
Risk Assessment · Profiling · Expert Testimony
Overconfidence Effect

People's confidence in their judgments consistently exceeds the accuracy of those judgments. Experts are often more overconfident than novices in their domains of expertise.

Clinical predictions of violence by experienced clinicians are no more accurate than actuarial tools — yet clinicians are more confident in their predictions.
Clinical Judgment · Actuarial vs. Clinical · Expert Credibility
Framing Effect

How information is presented changes how it is evaluated — even when the underlying data is identical. "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" are equivalent but produce different responses.

"This defendant reoffends 30% of the time" vs. "This defendant does not reoffend 70% of the time" — same fact, different jury impact.
Expert Communication · Risk Communication · Attorney Strategy
Illusory Correlation

Perceiving a relationship between variables when no statistically significant relationship exists. Particularly dangerous when built on memorable but non-representative cases.

Believing mental illness causes violence because violent mentally ill individuals receive more media coverage than the much larger population of non-violent mentally ill individuals.
Mental Illness Stigma · Risk Research · Public Policy
Sunk Cost Fallacy

Continuing to invest in a course of action because of prior investment — even when current evidence suggests it is no longer the best choice. Irrational continuation driven by loss aversion.

Prosecutors continuing to pursue a weak case because of the time and resources already invested, rather than dismissing it in the interest of justice.
Prosecutorial Decision-Making · Wrongful Conviction Risk
In-Group Bias

Favoring members of one's own group over members of out-groups. Operates automatically and unconsciously. Affects jurors, law enforcement, evaluators, and judges alike.

Jurors show greater leniency for defendants who share their race, religion, or social background — a robust and well-documented effect across cultures.
Jury Selection · Voir Dire · Sentencing Disparity
