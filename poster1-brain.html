The Brain — Structure,
Function & Forensic Relevance

Every forensic evaluation touches neuroscience. Understanding what each brain region does — and what happens when it's damaged, diseased, or underdeveloped — is foundational to criminal responsibility, risk assessment, and competency evaluation.

01

The Four Lobes of the Cerebral Cortex

Where higher cognition lives
Frontal Lobe
Front of skull — largest lobe

Functions: Executive function, impulse control, decision-making, planning, working memory, moral reasoning, personality expression, voluntary movement (motor cortex), speech production (Broca's area).

Impaired judgment, loss of impulse control, personality changes, aggression, inability to plan or sequence behavior. Phineas Gage is the classic case.
The most forensically critical lobe. Adolescent frontal lobe immaturity (not fully developed until ~age 25) is central to arguments against harsh juvenile sentencing. Frontal damage is cited in diminished capacity defenses, and frontal dysfunction appears consistently in psychopathy research (PCL-R scores correlate with reduced frontal activity).
Parietal Lobe
Top and rear of skull

Functions: Sensory integration, spatial awareness, attention, body image, mathematical processing, reading comprehension. Contains the somatosensory cortex (touch, pain, temperature mapping).

Neglect syndrome (ignoring one side of body/space), difficulty reading, spatial disorientation, chronic pain processing errors.
Relevant in traumatic brain injury (TBI) evaluations following assaults. Spatial processing deficits can affect competency assessments. Chronic pain claims in personal injury litigation often involve parietal processing evaluation.
Temporal Lobe
Sides of skull, above ears

Functions: Auditory processing, language comprehension (Wernicke's area), memory formation (via hippocampus), face recognition, emotional processing, long-term memory storage.

Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but nonsensical speech), memory loss, face blindness, auditory hallucinations, personality changes, temporal lobe epilepsy.
Temporal lobe epilepsy has historically been raised (controversially) in automatism defenses. Wernicke's aphasia can mimic psychosis. Memory formation deficits are central to amnesia claims in criminal cases — evaluators must distinguish genuine from feigned memory loss.
Occipital Lobe
Rear of skull

Functions: Visual processing — color, motion, depth, shape recognition. Everything you "see" is processed here after signals travel from the eyes via the optic nerves.

Visual field deficits, cortical blindness, visual hallucinations, inability to recognize objects (visual agnosia).
Visual hallucinations from occipital damage can be mistaken for psychosis. Relevant in evaluating the reliability of eyewitness testimony when witnesses have visual processing disorders. Also appears in TBI litigation after head impacts.
02

Key Deep Brain Structures

The limbic system and subcortical regions
Limbic System
Amygdala

Fear processing, threat detection, emotional memory. Hyperactive amygdala = heightened fear/aggression response. Hypoactive = reduced fear, seen in psychopathy.

Psychopathy Research · Fear Response · PTSD
Limbic System
Hippocampus

Converts short-term memories to long-term. Damaged by chronic stress, alcohol, PTSD. Critical to evaluating claimed amnesia in criminal cases.

Memory Claims · PTSD Evaluation · Competency
Limbic System
Hypothalamus

Regulates hunger, thirst, temperature, sexual drive, circadian rhythms. Links nervous system to endocrine system. Dysregulation affects mood and impulse control.

Impulse Control · Hormonal Factors
Limbic System
Cingulate Cortex

Error detection, emotional regulation, pain processing, decision-making. Anterior cingulate dysfunction linked to impulsivity and poor inhibition.

Impulse Control Research · Risk Assessment
Reward System
Nucleus Accumbens

Dopamine reward center. Central to addiction, motivation, and pleasure. Dysregulation drives compulsive behavior including substance abuse and risk-taking.

Addiction Defenses · Impulse Disorders
Brainstem
Prefrontal-Limbic Circuit

The regulatory relationship between the prefrontal cortex (brakes) and amygdala (accelerator). Forensic violence research focuses heavily on this balance.

Violence Risk · Criminal Responsibility
Motor
Cerebellum

Coordinates movement, balance, fine motor skills. Also involved in cognitive timing. Alcohol affects cerebellum first — explains impaired coordination in DUI.

DUI Evaluation · TBI Assessment
Relay
Thalamus

Sensory relay station — routes all incoming sensory data (except smell) to cortex. Thalamic damage can produce severe cognitive and sensory disturbances.

TBI Litigation · Sensory Processing

Neurotransmitters & Their Forensic Significance

Brain chemistry in the courtroom
Neurotransmitter Primary Role Deficit / Excess Effect Forensic Application
Dopamine Reward, motivation, movement, attention Deficit → depression, Parkinson's; Excess → psychosis, schizophrenia Schizophrenia defenses · Addiction · ADHD in juvenile evals
Serotonin Mood, impulse control, sleep, aggression Low serotonin strongly linked to impulsive aggression and violent behavior Violence risk research · Antidepressant defenses · Homicide evals
Norepinephrine Arousal, fight-or-flight, attention Excess → hyperarousal, anxiety, PTSD symptom maintenance PTSD evaluations · Battered woman syndrome · Stress defenses
GABA Primary inhibitory neurotransmitter — calms neural activity Deficit → anxiety, seizures; Alcohol enhances GABA (explains sedation) Alcohol intoxication defenses · Anxiety disorder evaluations
Glutamate Primary excitatory neurotransmitter — drives neural firing Excess causes neurotoxicity; dysregulation linked to psychosis TBI neurodegeneration · Psychosis evaluation
Oxytocin Social bonding, trust, empathy, attachment Low oxytocin linked to reduced empathy and social disengagement Psychopathy research · Attachment disorders in juvenile cases

Brain Development Timeline — Forensic Implications

Why age matters in criminal law
Age Range Development Stage What's Still Maturing Forensic / Legal Implication
Birth–5 Rapid synaptic growth Basic sensory and motor systems; early emotional regulation Early trauma effects on attachment and amygdala development
6–11 Synaptic pruning begins Language, memory consolidation, early executive function Competency to testify as child witness; trauma response evaluation
12–17 Adolescent remodeling Prefrontal cortex still highly immature; limbic system drives behavior Reduced culpability arguments; SAVRY use; Roper v. Simmons (2005) abolished juvenile death penalty citing brain dev.
18–24 Late adolescent development Prefrontal cortex still pruning; impulse control still developing Miller v. Alabama (2012); emerging adult sentencing reform arguments
25+ Full adult maturity Prefrontal cortex reaches full development (~age 25) Standard adult criminal responsibility framework applies
