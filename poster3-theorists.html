Psychological Theorists
Timeline — Freud to Now

Every major theory in psychology has roots in a specific thinker at a specific moment in history. Knowing who built what — and why — is the foundation of every undergraduate psych degree.

1856–1930s

The Founders — Psychoanalytic & Early Experimental

Building the field from scratch
1856–1939 Psychoanalysis
Sigmund Freud
Austrian neurologist · Father of Psychoanalysis

Proposed the unconscious mind as the primary driver of behavior. Developed the id, ego, and superego model. Introduced defense mechanisms, free association, and the talking cure.

Core IdeaUnconscious conflicts rooted in early development drive adult behavior. Bringing them to conscious awareness produces healing.
Defense mechanisms (repression, projection, rationalization) appear in forensic reports to explain why individuals deny or minimize criminal behavior. Psychodynamic understanding of early trauma informs treatment planning.
1875–1961 Analytical Psychology
Carl Jung
Swiss psychiatrist · Freud's student-turned-rival

Expanded unconscious to include the collective unconscious and archetypes. Introduced the concepts of introversion and extroversion, and the psychological shadow.

Core IdeaThe shadow contains the parts of ourselves we reject — unacknowledged, they project outward and drive destructive behavior.
The shadow concept is used clinically in understanding how criminals externalize blame. Introversion/extroversion framework influenced personality assessment tools used in forensic evaluation.
1870–1937 Individual Psychology
Alfred Adler
Austrian psychiatrist · Freud's student-turned-rival

Introduced the inferiority complex and compensatory striving for superiority. Focused on social interest and birth order as drivers of personality and behavior.

Core IdeaPeople are primarily motivated by social belonging and a desire to overcome feelings of inferiority. Unhealthy striving produces destructive behavior.
Inferiority → compensatory aggression is a common clinical framework for understanding violence motivated by perceived humiliation or disrespect. Relevant in gang violence and domestic violence research.
1849–1936 Classical Conditioning
Ivan Pavlov
Russian physiologist · Nobel Prize 1904

Discovered classical conditioning through his famous dog experiments. Showed that neutral stimuli could be paired with natural stimuli to produce conditioned responses.

Core IdeaBehavior is learned through association. A neutral stimulus paired repeatedly with an unconditioned stimulus comes to produce the same response.
Foundation for understanding how trauma triggers are formed (PTSD). Used in treatment of phobias, sex offender treatment programs, and understanding conditioned fear responses in abuse victims.
1900s–1960s

Behaviorism & Humanistic Psychology

Reaction against psychoanalysis — two different directions
1878–1958 Behaviorism
John B. Watson
American psychologist · Father of Behaviorism

Argued psychology should only study observable behavior — not the mind. Famous for the "Little Albert" experiment demonstrating conditioned emotional responses in humans.

Core IdeaGive me a dozen healthy infants and I can train any one of them to become any type of specialist — doctor, lawyer, thief. Environment determines behavior.
Nature vs. nurture debate central to criminal responsibility arguments. Watson's extreme environmentalism influenced early rehabilitation-focused corrections philosophy.
1904–1990 Operant Conditioning
B.F. Skinner
American psychologist · Radical Behaviorist

Developed operant conditioning through reinforcement and punishment schedules. Introduced the Skinner box. Argued free will is an illusion — all behavior is shaped by consequences.

Core IdeaBehavior is a function of its consequences. Reinforce a behavior and it increases. Punish it and it decreases. This is all the explanation you need.
Token economy systems in prisons and psychiatric hospitals. Behavior modification in sex offender treatment. The deterrence theory of criminal justice is grounded in Skinnerian punishment principles.
1908–1970 Humanistic Psychology
Abraham Maslow
American psychologist · Hierarchy of Needs

Proposed the hierarchy of needs — a five-tier pyramid from physiological needs at the base to self-actualization at the top. Argued humans are inherently motivated toward growth.

Core IdeaPeople cannot pursue higher-level psychological needs (belonging, esteem, self-actualization) when lower-level survival needs are unmet.
Explains criminogenic environments — poverty, homelessness, and physical insecurity prevent self-regulatory development. Maslow's framework underlies many rehabilitation and reentry program designs.
1902–1987 Person-Centered Therapy
Carl Rogers
American psychologist · Humanistic therapy pioneer

Developed person-centered (client-centered) therapy. Core conditions: unconditional positive regard, empathy, and genuineness. Believed all people have an innate drive toward growth.

Core IdeaGiven the right relational conditions, people will naturally move toward health, growth, and self-understanding. The therapist creates the conditions; the client does the work.
Therapeutic alliance principles foundational to forensic treatment settings. Motivational Interviewing (used heavily in corrections) is directly descended from Rogerian principles.
1960s–1990s

Cognitive Revolution & Social Learning

The mind comes back to psychology
1925–present Social Learning Theory
Albert Bandura
Canadian-American psychologist · Stanford University

Demonstrated observational learning through the Bobo doll experiments. Showed children imitate aggressive behavior they observe. Introduced self-efficacy as a core motivational concept.

Core IdeaPeople learn by observing others. Behavior is influenced by what we observe being reinforced or punished in others — not only in ourselves.
Foundational to understanding how children learn violence in abusive households. Media violence research draws on Bandura. Self-efficacy is central to recidivism reduction programs and correctional treatment.
1924–1997 Cognitive Psychology
Aaron Beck
American psychiatrist · Father of CBT

Developed Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) originally for depression. Identified automatic thoughts, cognitive distortions, and schemas as core drivers of psychological distress.

Core IdeaIt is not events themselves that disturb us, but our interpretations of them. Change the thinking; change the emotional and behavioral response.
CBT is the most evidence-based treatment for offender populations. Cognitive distortions (minimization, victim blaming, entitlement thinking) are specifically targeted in sex offender and domestic violence treatment programs.
1896–1980 Cognitive Development
Jean Piaget
Swiss psychologist · Father of developmental psychology

Mapped cognitive development through four stages: Sensorimotor, Preoperational, Concrete Operational, and Formal Operational. Showed children think qualitatively differently than adults.

Core IdeaChildren are not mini-adults. They construct understanding of the world through distinct developmental stages — each with its own logic and limitations.
Developmental stage determines competency to understand legal proceedings (child witnesses, juvenile defendants). Piaget's framework underpins legal standards for children's testimony and juvenile competency evaluations.
1902–1994 Psychosocial Development
Erik Erikson
German-American psychologist · 8 Stages of Development

Proposed eight psychosocial stages across the entire lifespan, each presenting a developmental crisis. Success builds psychological strength; failure leaves vulnerability.

Core IdeaDevelopment continues across the entire lifespan. Each stage presents a conflict; resolution builds a specific ego strength. Unresolved crises create vulnerability and dysfunction.
Failure at Industry vs. Inferiority (age 6–12) and Identity vs. Role Confusion (adolescence) are particularly relevant in understanding juvenile delinquency and the developmental roots of criminal behavior.
1970s–Present

Forensic Psychology Pioneers

The researchers who built the field you're entering
1944–present Cognitive Psychology / Law
Elizabeth Loftus
American cognitive psychologist · UC Irvine

Revolutionized understanding of eyewitness testimony through misinformation effect research. Demonstrated that memory is reconstructive, not reproductive — and easily contaminated by post-event information.

Core IdeaMemory is not a recording. It is a reconstruction. Every time we recall something, we risk altering it. Leading questions plant false memories with surprising ease.
Her research directly changed jury instructions, eyewitness identification procedures, and interrogation guidelines in the United States. She is the most cited female psychologist alive. She testified in hundreds of trials including O.J. Simpson and Harvey Weinstein.
1943–present Forensic Psychology / Psychopathy
Robert Hare
Canadian psychologist · UBC · Creator of PCL-R

Developed the Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R) — the gold standard instrument for measuring psychopathy. Decades of research distinguishing psychopathy from ASPD and identifying its neurological correlates.

Core IdeaPsychopathy is a distinct clinical construct characterized by interpersonal-affective features (shallow emotion, lack of empathy) combined with chronic antisocial behavior — and it predicts violence above all other variables.
The PCL-R is used in virtually every serious forensic violence risk evaluation worldwide. Hare's research on psychopathy and recidivism directly influences parole decisions, dangerous offender designations, and sentencing arguments across North America.
1954–present Forensic Psychology / Risk Assessment
John Monahan
American psychologist · University of Virginia School of Law

Pioneered empirical research on violence risk prediction. Led the MacArthur Violence Risk Assessment Study — the most comprehensive study of community violence by people with mental disorders ever conducted.

Core IdeaViolence risk is not a fixed trait — it is dynamic, multifactorial, and predictable within statistical limits. The goal is structured professional judgment, not crystal-ball prediction.
His work directly dismantled the myth that mental illness = violence. MacArthur findings show substance abuse is a far stronger violence predictor than mental illness alone. His structured risk assessment framework is used in civil commitment and release decisions.
1937–present Forensic Psychology / Assessment
Richard Rogers
American psychologist · University of North Texas

Leading authority on malingering, response styles, and forensic assessment. Developer of the SIRS-2 (gold standard for malingering detection) and prolific researcher on competency, insanity, and Miranda rights.

Core IdeaForensic evaluators have a professional and ethical obligation to rigorously assess response style — because defendants have strong incentives to distort their presentation in either direction.
SIRS-2 is standard in virtually all serious forensic evaluations. His research on Miranda rights comprehension showed that a substantial portion of defendants — especially juveniles and those with low IQ — do not genuinely understand their rights when waiving them.
