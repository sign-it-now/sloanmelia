Violence & Recidivism Risk Assessment Tools

Predicting future behavior from scored data
PCL-R · Hare Psychopathy Checklist-Revised
Psychopathy Checklist-Revised

20-item clinician-rated scale measuring psychopathic personality traits. Score 0–40. Scored via file review + structured interview. Gold standard for psychopathy assessment in forensic settings.

Correlation Analysis Inter-rater Reliability Sentencing · Parole
Does this individual's score predict violent recidivism above base rate for this population?
HCR-20 · Historical Clinical Risk Management
HCR-20 V3

20-item structured professional judgment tool. Divided into Historical (H), Clinical (C), and Risk Management (R) subscales. Used to assess and manage violence risk — not predict it mechanically.

SPJ Framework Base Rate Comparison Civil Commitment · Release
What dynamic factors currently elevate or reduce this person's risk of future violence?
STATIC-99R · Sex Offender Risk
STATIC-99R

10-item actuarial tool for estimating sexual recidivism risk in adult males. Uses static (unchanging) historical factors. Compares individual score to normative recidivism rates from large samples.

Actuarial / Base Rates AUC / ROC Curves Sex Offender Eval
How does this individual's recidivism probability compare to the reference group at 5 and 10 years?
LSI-R · Level of Service Inventory-Revised
LSI-R

54-item structured interview assessing criminogenic needs across 10 domains including criminal history, education, finances, family, and attitudes. Used heavily in corrections for case planning.

Regression Analysis Predictive Validity Corrections · Probation
Which specific need areas, if addressed, would most reduce general recidivism risk?
VRAG-R · Violence Risk Appraisal Guide-Revised
VRAG-R

12-item actuarial instrument estimating probability of violent recidivism over 5- and 12-year follow-up periods. One of the most validated actuarial instruments in the field.

Probability Bins Sensitivity / Specificity Dangerous Offender Hearings
Into which actuarial risk bin does this individual fall, and what is the associated recidivism probability?
SAVRY · Structured Assessment of Violence Risk in Youth
SAVRY

24-item structured professional judgment tool for adolescents ages 12–18. Includes historical, social/contextual, and individual risk factors plus protective factors. Developmentally sensitive.

SPJ Framework Protective Factor Weighting Juvenile Courts
How do this youth's protective factors offset identified risk factors in the current developmental context?
02

Competency & Criminal Responsibility Tools

Measuring legal capacity and mental state at time of offense
MacCAT-CA · MacArthur Competence Assessment Tool
MacCAT-CA

22-item structured interview assessing three competency abilities: Understanding (legal concepts), Appreciation (how law applies to their situation), and Reasoning (ability to weigh information). Yields normative scores.

Normative Comparison Standard Scores Competency Hearings
Does this defendant understand the charges and can they meaningfully assist in their own defense?
ECST-R · Evaluation of Competency to Stand Trial-Revised
ECST-R

Structured interview covering three Dusky prongs: Factual Understanding, Rational Understanding, and Consult with Counsel. Also includes feigning scales to detect malingering of incompetence.

Scale Scores Feigning Detection Competency Restoration
Is the defendant's reported incompetence genuine or feigned, and which specific Dusky prong is impaired?
RCRAS · Rogers Criminal Responsibility Assessment Scales
RCRAS

Quantifies criminal responsibility by rating 30 variables across psychological impairment, cognitive control, and behavioral control at time of the offense. Provides a decision model for legal insanity.

Decision Algorithm Multi-variable Rating Insanity Defense
At the time of the offense, did a mental disease substantially impair the defendant's cognitive or behavioral control?
03

Personality Assessment & Malingering Detection

Who is this person — and are they telling the truth?
MMPI-3 · Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory
MMPI-3

335-item self-report personality inventory with extensive validity scales (TRIN, VRIN, F, L, K). The most widely researched personality test in forensic settings. Produces T-scores normalized against large normative samples.

T-Scores / Normal Distribution Validity Scales All Forensic Evals
Does this profile reflect genuine psychopathology or response bias, and how does it compare to relevant forensic normative groups?
PAI · Personality Assessment Inventory
PAI

344-item self-report with 22 scales including clinical, treatment, interpersonal, and validity scales. Commonly used alongside MMPI-3. Particularly useful for treatment planning in correctional settings.

T-Scores Subscale Profiles Treatment Planning · Corrections
What clinical presentation and treatment targets emerge, and does the validity profile suggest overreporting or underreporting?
SIRS-2 · Structured Interview of Reported Symptoms
SIRS-2

172-item structured interview with 8 primary and 5 supplementary scales specifically designed to detect feigned mental disorders. Considered the gold standard for malingering assessment in forensic evaluations.

Sensitivity / Specificity Classification Accuracy Competency · Disability Claims
Is there evidence of feigned or exaggerated psychiatric symptoms that would undermine the credibility of the self-reported presentation?
TOMM · Test of Memory Malingering
TOMM

Visual recognition memory test with two learning trials and a retention trial. Uses forced-choice methodology — genuine memory-impaired individuals statistically should still score above chance. Below-chance performance indicates feigning.

Binomial Probability Chance Performance Baseline Neuropsychological Evals
Does the individual's memory performance fall below statistical chance, indicating deliberate failure rather than genuine impairment?

Core Statistical Formulas in Forensic Psychology

The math behind every expert opinion
Diagnostic Accuracy
Sensitivity
TP ÷ (TP + FN)

How well does the tool catch actual positives? High sensitivity = few missed offenders. Critical for violence risk tools.

Diagnostic Accuracy
Specificity
TN ÷ (TN + FP)

How well does the tool avoid false alarms? High specificity = few innocent people flagged. Critical for courtroom testimony.

Predictive Value
Positive Predictive Value
TP ÷ (TP + FP)

If the tool says "high risk," how often is it right? Varies dramatically with base rate of the behavior in the population.

Overall Accuracy
AUC (Area Under Curve)
ROC curve integral

Overall discriminative accuracy. AUC = 0.5 is chance; 0.7 = acceptable; 0.8+ = strong. Most forensic tools fall 0.68–0.78.

Hypothesis Testing
Chi-Square
Σ (O − E)² ÷ E

Tests whether observed frequencies differ from expected. Used in profiling research to test whether demographic patterns exceed base rates.

Agreement
Cohen's Kappa
(Po − Pe) ÷ (1 − Pe)

Inter-rater reliability beyond chance. Tools like PCL-R must demonstrate adequate kappa (≥0.70) to be admissible as reliable instruments.

Effect Size
Cohen's d
(M₁ − M₂) ÷ SDpooled

Standardized difference between two group means. Used to report how much offenders differ from normative population on any measure.

Relationship Strength
Pearson's r
Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) ÷ n·Sx·Sy

Correlation between scores and outcomes. Used to validate that risk tool scores correlate with actual recidivism data across studies.

Error Types
Type I Error (α)
P(reject H₀ | H₀ true)

False positive rate. In forensic terms: labeling a non-dangerous person as dangerous. Ethical and legal consequences for the defendant.

Error Types
Type II Error (β)
P(fail reject H₀ | H₀ false)

False negative rate. In forensic terms: failing to identify a dangerous person. Consequences for public safety. The tradeoff haunts every risk eval.

Cognitive Biases That Corrupt Forensic Conclusions

What statistics is designed to protect against
Stat Threat: Sampling Bias
Confirmation Bias

Seeking and interpreting evidence that confirms a pre-existing hypothesis. The primary threat to profiling validity. Undetected in non-blind evaluations.

Stat Threat: Base Rate Neglect
Prosecution Bias

Unconsciously framing findings to support the referring party. Requires evaluator to be blind to referral source where possible. Corrected by actuarial tools.

Stat Threat: Anchoring Error
Hindsight Bias

Knowing the outcome changes perceived probability of that outcome. Particularly dangerous in criminal responsibility evaluations. Skews retrospective risk judgments.

Stat Threat: Ecological Fallacy
Group-to-Individual Error

Applying group-level statistics (e.g. "STATIC-99R score X recidivates at 30%") directly to an individual without adjustment for idiographic factors.

Stat Threat: Overconfidence
Illusory Correlation

Perceiving a relationship between variables (e.g. behavior and diagnosis) when none exists at statistically significant levels. Requires null hypothesis testing to expose.

Stat Threat: Invalid Profiles
Feedback Loop Bias

Profiles built from biased conviction data (where bias already shaped who was arrested and charged) create a self-validating cycle. The DC Sniper case is the textbook example.
