Biology & Neuroscience —
The Body Behind the Mind

You cannot evaluate a brain without understanding biology. Every diagnosis, every risk factor, every medication effect, and every criminal responsibility argument eventually traces back to biology. This is the foundation under everything else.

01

The Nervous System — Core Architecture

What every psych student must know cold
Central Nervous System
Brain + Spinal Cord

The CNS processes all incoming information and coordinates responses. The brain is divided into the cerebrum (higher cognition), cerebellum (coordination), and brainstem (autonomic survival functions: breathing, heart rate, arousal).

TBI can damage any CNS structure. Forensic evaluators must understand which functions are impaired by which injuries — frontal TBI produces very different deficits than temporal or cerebellar TBI.
Peripheral Nervous System
Somatic + Autonomic

The PNS connects the CNS to the body. The somatic PNS controls voluntary movement. The autonomic PNS regulates involuntary functions and splits into sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) branches.

The sympathetic nervous system response (elevated heart rate, cortisol release, hypervigilance) is the physiological basis of the PTSD arousal cluster. Polygraph testing attempts to measure peripheral autonomic responses — and fails because these responses are not lie-specific.
Neuron Structure
How Nerve Cells Communicate

Neurons have dendrites (receive signals), a cell body (soma), and an axon (transmits signals). Action potentials travel down the axon. At the synapse, neurotransmitters are released into the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the receiving neuron.

Psychoactive substances and medications work by altering synaptic transmission — blocking reuptake (SSRIs), mimicking neurotransmitters (opioids), blocking receptors (antipsychotics), or flooding the synapse (stimulants). Understanding this mechanism is essential for evaluating substance-related defenses.
Endocrine System
Hormones and Behavior

The endocrine system communicates through hormones released into the bloodstream. Key glands: adrenal glands (cortisol, adrenaline), pituitary (master gland), gonads (testosterone, estrogen), thyroid (metabolism, mood).

Testosterone has a modest but real correlation with aggression and dominance behavior. Cortisol dysregulation is a biological marker of chronic stress and trauma. Thyroid disorders can produce psychiatric symptoms (psychosis, depression, anxiety) that must be ruled out in differential diagnosis.
Genetics
Nature in the Nature vs. Nurture Debate

Behavioral genetics examines the heritability of psychological traits through twin studies, adoption studies, and molecular genetics. Heritability estimates for personality disorders range from 40–60%. No single "crime gene" exists — genetic influence is polygenic and probabilistic.

Genetic evidence (MAOA "warrior gene" studies, twin studies of antisocial behavior) has been introduced in capital sentencing mitigation — with significant scientific controversy. Evaluators must understand both the evidence and its severe limitations before it becomes expert testimony.
Epigenetics
How Experience Changes Gene Expression

Epigenetics studies how environmental experiences turn genes on or off without changing the underlying DNA sequence. Childhood trauma, stress, and substance exposure all produce epigenetic changes — some of which can be transmitted across generations.

Epigenetic research on ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences) is increasingly relevant in forensic mitigation arguments. The biological embedding of early trauma — measurable in gene methylation patterns — provides a biological mechanism connecting childhood abuse to adult criminal behavior.

The ACE Score Finding

The original CDC-Kaiser ACE Study (Felitti et al., 1998) found that ACEs are dose-responsive — each additional ACE category significantly increases the probability of mental illness, substance abuse, chronic disease, and involvement in the criminal justice system. An ACE score of 4+ is associated with dramatically elevated risk across every negative health and behavioral outcome measured. This data is now central to trauma-informed forensic evaluation and sentencing reform arguments.
