Developmental Psychology —
Piaget, Erikson & Kohlberg on One Chart

Development across the lifespan is foundational to understanding criminal behavior, competency, culpability, and treatment. These three frameworks — cognitive, psychosocial, and moral — are the backbone of every developmental psych course and appear constantly in forensic evaluations of juveniles and adults.

Jean Piaget · 1896–1980

Cognitive Development — Four Stages

How we think changes qualitatively with age
Stage Age Core Cognitive Ability Key Limitations Forensic Relevance
Sensorimotor 0–2 yrs Learning through senses and motor actions. Develops object permanence (~8 months) — things exist even when not seen. No symbolic thought. No language-based understanding. No capacity for abstract concepts. Establishes why infants cannot be witnesses. Early sensorimotor deprivation (neglect, abuse) disrupts foundational cognitive architecture — relevant in developmental trauma evaluations.
Preoperational 2–7 yrs Symbolic thinking emerges. Language develops rapidly. Pretend play. Beginning of logical thought. Egocentric thinking (cannot take another's perspective). No conservation. Animism. Cannot follow logical sequences. Children in this stage cannot reliably distinguish their own perspective from another's — critical for evaluating the reliability of child witness testimony. Suggestibility is highest in this stage.
Concrete Operational 7–11 yrs Logical thinking about concrete objects. Conservation mastered. Decentration. Understanding of reversibility. Cannot yet reason abstractly. Logic requires concrete examples. Hypothetical reasoning is not yet available. Children can testify more reliably but still require concrete, non-abstract questioning. Evaluators avoid hypothetical questions. This stage marks the beginning of genuine moral understanding of rules.
Formal Operational 12+ yrs Abstract reasoning. Hypothetical-deductive thinking. Can reason about possibilities, not just realities. Metacognition. Not all adolescents or adults fully reach this stage. Emotional arousal impairs formal operational thinking even in those who possess it. Full formal operational thinking is required to genuinely understand legal proceedings and assist in one's defense — competency standard. Adolescent brains reach formal operations cognitively before emotional regulation catches up. This gap is central to juvenile sentencing arguments.
Erik Erikson · 1902–1994

Psychosocial Development — Eight Stages

Each stage presents a crisis whose resolution shapes the next
Stage / Crisis Age Successful Resolution Failed Resolution Forensic Relevance
Trust vs. Mistrust 0–18 mo Hope. Sense of security. Trusting caregivers and the world as fundamentally safe. Pervasive mistrust. Fear. Difficulty forming secure attachments throughout life. Attachment disruption at this stage — from abuse, neglect, or inconsistent caregiving — is the earliest developmental risk factor for later antisocial behavior and personality pathology.
Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt 18 mo–3 yrs Will. Sense of self-control and autonomy. Confidence in own abilities. Shame. Self-doubt. Excessive control needs. Rigidity or impulsivity. Early trauma and harsh parenting at this stage produce patterns of shame that drive adult behavior — including rage responses to perceived humiliation (relevant in assault evaluations).
Initiative vs. Guilt 3–6 yrs Purpose. Ability to initiate activities. Sense of direction and meaning. Guilt. Inhibited initiation. Fear of punishment for assertiveness. Excessive guilt from harsh punishment can produce inhibited personality styles. Insufficient guilt (from permissive parenting or absent moral guidance) contributes to entitlement and antisocial trajectories.
Industry vs. Inferiority 6–12 yrs Competence. Mastery of skills. Sense of capability in social and academic domains. Inferiority. Sense of inadequacy compared to peers. Withdrawal from challenges. School failure and peer rejection at this stage are significant predictors of delinquency. Feeling incompetent in legitimate domains pushes children toward criminal peer groups where they can achieve status and competence.
Identity vs. Role Confusion 12–18 yrs Fidelity. Stable sense of identity — who am I? Coherent self-concept. Role confusion. Unstable identity. Susceptibility to negative peer influence. Identity foreclosure. The most forensically critical stage. Adolescent identity formation is the mechanism through which gang membership, criminal identity adoption, and peer criminality operate. Unresolved identity crisis is central to juvenile assessment.
Intimacy vs. Isolation 18–40 yrs Love. Capacity for deep, committed relationships. Genuine intimacy without losing self. Isolation. Superficial relationships. Fear of intimacy. Loneliness driving maladaptive coping. Intimate partner violence perpetrators frequently show failures at this stage — seeking control as a substitute for genuine intimacy. Stalking behavior often emerges from inability to tolerate the anxiety of separation in this stage.
Generativity vs. Stagnation 40–65 yrs Care. Investment in future generations. Productivity. Legacy-building. Stagnation. Self-absorption. Sense of meaninglessness. Mid-life crisis behavior. Mid-life offending (often white-collar crime or late-onset DUI) sometimes reflects stagnation — attempts to manufacture excitement or significance. Relevant in evaluating first-time adult offenders with no prior criminal history.
Integrity vs. Despair 65+ yrs Wisdom. Sense of meaning and acceptance reviewing one's life. Integration of experience. Despair. Regret. Fear of death. Bitterness about unchosen paths. Elder abuse evaluations, testamentary capacity assessments, and competency to stand trial for elderly defendants all engage this stage. Despair-driven late-life offending or self-neglect may have forensic implications.
Lawrence Kohlberg · 1927–1987

Moral Development — Three Levels, Six Stages

How moral reasoning evolves from self-interest to principle
Level / Stage Age (approx) Moral Reasoning Basis Example Reasoning Forensic Relevance
Pre-Conventional
Stage 1: Obedience		 ~4–7 Avoid punishment. Rules are obeyed to escape negative consequences from authority. "I won't steal because I'll get in trouble." Many adult offenders reason at this level. Deterrence-only criminal justice policy assumes all offenders reason at Stage 1. Risk/reward miscalculation, not moral absence, drives most crime at this level.
Pre-Conventional
Stage 2: Self-Interest		 ~7–10 Rules followed when they serve one's interests. Reciprocal exchange: "You scratch my back..." "I'll help you if you help me. Otherwise, why bother?" Instrumental aggression, transactional criminal relationships, and gang economic arrangements are grounded in Stage 2 reasoning. Antisocial individuals often function primarily at this level regardless of age.
Conventional
Stage 3: Social Conformity		 ~10–adolescence Be a good person. Live up to social roles. Approval from significant others matters. "I want to be a good person. What would my family think?" Peer conformity is the engine of adolescent offending at this stage — not moral deficiency but social conformity to delinquent peer norms. Understanding this is foundational to juvenile intervention design.
Conventional
Stage 4: Law and Order		 adolescence+ Maintain social order. Laws represent the social contract; obeying them preserves society. "I follow the law because society depends on everyone following it." Most law-abiding adults operate at Stage 4. Understanding why some individuals fail to reach this stage — and what developmental factors prevented it — is central to forensic evaluation of adult offenders.
Post-Conventional
Stage 5: Social Contract		 adulthood (not all) Laws are social contracts that should serve justice. Unjust laws should be challenged through legitimate means. "This law is unjust. I should work to change it through democratic means." Civil disobedience, whistleblowing, and conscientious objection cases engage Stage 5 reasoning. Evaluators may need to assess whether a defendant's law violation reflected principled Stage 5 reasoning or self-serving rationalization.
Post-Conventional
Stage 6: Universal Ethics		 rare Universal ethical principles — justice, dignity, equality — override specific laws. Laws that violate these principles must be resisted. "I would hide Jewish families from the Nazis regardless of the law." Kohlberg considered Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. to operate at Stage 6. Extremely rare. Forensic evaluators occasionally encounter defendants who genuinely believe their criminal acts were morally required — requiring careful assessment of principle vs. delusion.
Urie Bronfenbrenner · 1917–2005

Ecological Systems Theory — The Environment of Development

Development happens in layered social contexts
Innermost Layer
Microsystem

The immediate environments the person directly experiences: family, school, peer group, neighborhood.

A child's day-to-day interactions with parents, teachers, and friends.

Family violence, school failure, and delinquent peer association operate at the microsystem level. Forensic risk assessments (LSI-R, SAVRY) measure microsystem factors directly.
Second Layer
Mesosystem

Interactions between microsystems — how family and school relate to each other, for example.

Parents who don't engage with school; disconnection between home and school norms.

Family-school disconnection is a risk factor for delinquency. Rehabilitation programs that fail to address mesosystem connections often produce poor outcomes.
Third Layer
Exosystem

Environments the person doesn't directly experience but that affect them: parent's workplace, community agencies, local economic conditions.

A parent's job loss leads to family stress that increases abuse risk.

Poverty, unemployment, and lack of social services operate at the exosystem level. These factors appear as background variables in forensic social history sections and mitigation arguments.
Outermost Layer
Macrosystem

The broader cultural, ideological, and social context: laws, cultural norms, political systems, economic structures.

Racial disparities in criminal justice. Cultural norms around violence and masculinity.

Systemic racism, mass incarceration policies, and structural inequality are macrosystem factors that forensic evaluators must consider when contextualizing individual criminal histories.
Time Dimension
Chronosystem

The influence of time and historical events on development — how transitions, traumas, and historical context shape experience across the lifespan.

A child who experiences a parent's incarceration at age 7 is affected differently than one who experiences it at 15.

Timing of adversity matters forensically. Age of onset of abuse, age of first offense, and developmental windows for intervention all involve chronosystem thinking.
